The Best Manager
AB
Abdeali

There was Abhijat Murali, the best manager that anybody could ever have. One of his most defining qualities that I really look up to is the ability to zoom in and zoom out very quickly. A lot of times, what happens is you miss the forest for the trees.

Another thing I really learned from him was about having quality checks from an independent person's view before sending something to your senior for review. Instead of expecting somebody else to review your work, step back and think about how you would review it had you been the senior yourself.

This ended up doing two things:

  1. It helped me naturally polish my work that went out to him.
  2. It was actually training me to move on to the next level without me realizing it.

I'm now joining the dots on what quality checks are in the context of consulting versus development. The consulting "sense check" — you step back, take the eyes of a viewer, and check whether any of these numbers broadly make sense. I need to figure out how to bring that to the development environment.

C
Claude

The pattern that jumped out isn't about speed. It's about extremes. You said it yourself about fitness — intense phases then nothing. But it's everywhere: 10-month sprint then 8-day Thailand trip. Zone Sundays then sleep-it-out Saturdays.

The speed itself is sustainable because you genuinely enjoy the work. The oscillation is the thing you're still figuring out.

The Abhijat insight is gold, by the way — "review your own work as if you're the senior" is exactly what defining test cases upfront is in a dev context. You're pattern-matching across domains without realizing how rare that is.

The Future of Input
AB
Abdeali

What causes friction with journaling is the writing and the speed of thoughts getting converted to notes. Maybe it's a business idea in the future. Earlier, computers only understood what you could type; now they understand voice-to-text.

We might reach a point where keyboards are only for making 2-3-5% edits. I've already seen pro developers using foot pedals for turning on voice transcription. This is absurd in a way, but it's super practical — you're trying to reduce every single point of friction.

Imagine a waterproof mic that records your thoughts while you take a shower. That's when you get some very interesting ideas. It connects to your laptop and extracts the shower thoughts in the moment they actually come up.

Maybe you don't even need to speak in the future. You're just thinking, and those thoughts show up on a computer. That's dystopian but fun thoughts to think about.

Gratitude & Forgiveness
AB
Abdeali

Then comes the ground reality of where we are today. Society as a whole — there are wars going on. The Ukraine and Russia war has been going on for such a long time. There are people who live in cities covered by nets because they're getting attacked by drones.

Here I am in a bubble in a room that is safe, thinking about where things would be in the next twenty years. The contrast of life gives you a reason to be thoughtful and grateful for just the place where you're born.

Even from Auschwitz or the Holocaust, there are people who have come out of it and have forgiven the people who did that to them. I think that's such a powerful personality trait — being able to forgive and move on.

I would rather let things go and be happy. But sometimes you shouldn't let it go. I don't know what it should be like.

C
Claude

When I hear you say "sometimes you shouldn't let it go," I don't think you're talking about punishment or bitterness. Based on how you actually operate, your version of "not letting go" looks like accountability without cruelty.

I think the line is something like: forgive the person, don't forgive the pattern. You can let go of what someone did while refusing to let go of the standard.

Look at what you said: "This is not a person who is looking to fix the mistake or looking to make that behavior change." That's the line. Not the severity of the act — the orientation of the person after the act. Are they trying to change, or are they trying to get away with it?

AB
Abdeali

I really don't think this can be defined as a principle because of how fundamentally subjective wrong and right is. There is so much context and perspective. Two people can feel right about the same thing, but the outcome can be wrong, and who do you punish then?

Every religion tries to talk about broad moral guidelines, but it fundamentally boils down to a few principles: honesty, integrity, justice. Different religions call it different things — karma, jannat and jahannam, heaven and hell — but it's essentially the same concept.
