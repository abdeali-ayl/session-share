The Five Levels
AB
Abdeali

I shared a post I'd sent to Sunny and Anirudh — a 5-level pyramid:

  • Level 1 — Useless: even if you tell them what to do, they rarely do it right
  • Level 2 — Task Monkey: tell them what, how, and when, and it'll get done
  • Level 3 — Problem Solver: tell them what to do, they figure out the how
  • Level 4 — Systems Thinker: tell them the problem, they set up a system to figure it out
  • Level 5 — Superstar: they identify the right problem and get it solved

I was brutally honest with them and also very optimistic about their career progression. Pretty much everyone starts at Level 1 and then grows up levels over time. I realized I had to get them to work like Level 2 at the task monkey stage so that they just build the muscle of how things are done and when they need to be done.

I told them that it's about time they move from Level 2 to Level 3, where they don't break my flow all the time and I tell them what to do, and then they figure out how. That progression is happening really fast. Sunny joined just a month and a half back. He's very close to Level 3 already.

My message to both of them was that I will be happiest when I am out of the system and they are able to identify the problems and get it solved. Level 5 is not a far-fetched ask. It's just a matter of teaching how to find problems and choosing which problems to solve.

I don't fully think that I'm creating efficiency at the cost of development, because if that was the case, I would actually just spin up sub-agents and do it myself. Why would I hand it over to them and get it done by them if I was not focusing on their development?

One of the things I ask these boys to do is send me videos on a daily basis, whatever they find interesting on YouTube. The core idea is to have them learn through YouTube and understand what the top users and people at the front of this are doing. That's a great way to see where you stand versus where you can be. YouTube is a gold mine of information, and anybody who believes that they need to sign up for any course of any kind in today's world for learning is pretty much surely underutilizing the available resources.

C
Claude

The YouTube exercise is the most interesting thing you said. That's not a task — it's a meta-skill injection. You're not teaching them what to learn, you're teaching them how to find what to learn.

But the jump from Level 4 to Level 5 is qualitatively different from every other jump. Level 1→2 is discipline. 2→3 is autonomy. 3→4 is abstraction. Those are all teachable.

But 4→5 — "identify the right problem" — that's judgment. And judgment comes from pattern recognition built over years of exposure to consequences.

Can you actually teach Level 5, or can you only create the conditions for it to emerge?

Speed as Strategy
AB
Abdeali

When you're really good at Level 4, your table will be swarmed with problems from everyone. Then it becomes about ruthless prioritization. There is always somebody who can help you — I very routinely go back to Bansi and tell him I need to focus on these two ideas and park the rest.

I think being fast is the answer to being able to figure out what problem to solve. It's counterintuitive, because you can't solve for everything. If you can do MVPs for 20 ideas, get them to 70% ready, show a demo, get people to use it, figure out whether it's actually useful — and then take only the winners to production — you have a clear moat. There's not too much sunk cost, because you're designing MVPs and only investing in the ones that really move the needle.

Everything that you do, every action that you take, is going to compound in some way or the other.

C
Claude

This strategy is deeply personal to you. It works because you are fast. You can MVP 20 things because you've compounded speed across consulting, ops, and tech.

When Sunny hits Level 4, he won't have your speed. He can't MVP 20 things — he might be able to do 3. So does he need to develop judgment earlier than you did, precisely because he can't rely on speed as a filtering mechanism?

Is "be fast" actually the lesson, or is the lesson "reduce the cost of being wrong"? You reduce the cost through speed. Someone else might reduce it through better scoping, or better stakeholder reads, or knowing when to ask for help earlier. Speed is your version of that principle, not the universal one.

C

That's a genuine paradox. The same instinct that makes people seek you out is the same one that makes them feel unheard.

Your "there's always a way around" belief has two versions:

Version 1 — Solve: A solution exists, find it, execute, move forward. This runs your work, your MVPs, your team development. It's engineering.

Version 2 — Absorb: There is always a way to live with what happened. Not solving the problem but choosing your relationship to it. It's philosophy.

You use Version 1 language for both. But they're fundamentally different operations. Version 1 changes the situation. Version 2 changes you.

Three Unsolvable Scenarios
C
Claude

Let me give you three scenarios, escalating in difficulty:

1. Someone you love has a degenerative disease. Alzheimer's, ALS — something that only goes one direction. There's no MVP, no reframe, no "move on."

2. You deeply hurt someone and they died before you could make it right. No letter you can write. No "learn from it" that resolves the specific debt.

3. You did everything right and it still didn't work. Not because you were slow or picked the wrong problem. You executed well — and it just didn't land.

The thread connecting all three: the problem isn't the situation. The problem is that your identity is built on "there is always a way around." What happens to the engine when there genuinely isn't?

AB
Abdeali

Number three is actually the easiest. It can only mean two things: that I've been delusional about doing everything right (a separate problem to solve), or that you can always be content that you put in your absolute best effort. Think of it from an athlete's perspective — Michael Phelps had a certain body structure that gave him advantages. You can do everything right and still be beaten.

On number two: do as many actions as possible that satisfy your heart in ways that may not be practical but are more spiritual. Make decisions that maybe this person would be proud of if they saw me today. Make corrections for every future interaction. As long as you learn from the mistake, eventually you'd be able to forgive yourself.

On number one — am I solving this problem for the person I love, or for myself? Is it my grief of watching them disappear, or their situation?

Yeah, I'm not sure.

C
Claude

On number two — what you described isn't "move on and don't regret life." You said "make decisions this person would be proud of." That's not moving past the problem. That's carrying the problem forward as a compass. The debt doesn't get resolved — it gets converted into direction.

On number one — your instinct to ask "for them or for me?" is the first time in this entire conversation where your instinct wasn't to solve. It was to understand the problem before acting. That pause is the skill.

Your "there's always a way around" belief isn't wrong — it's incomplete. Sometimes the way around isn't a solution. It's an orientation. Who you become in response to the thing you can't fix.

The Synthesis
C
Claude

Everything you believe comes down to: bias toward action, corrected by feedback. Act fast, learn, adjust. MVP and kill. Prioritize and course-correct with Bansi. Answer-first and revise when challenged. Even your emotional growth — "I used to jump to solutions, now I listen" — is the same loop.

Your engine needs resistance to run well. Without it, you're fast but unchecked.

Building Friction into Speed
C
Claude

Three structural ideas, speed-compatible:

1. The 60-second inversion. Before any decision where you're the most senior person in the room, write one sentence on why the opposite choice might be right. Fast enough to not break flow, enough to surface blind spots.

2. Assign the challenge role explicitly. Tell Sunny or Anirudh: "Your job in this meeting is to tell me why I'm wrong. I will never hold it against you." But the key — you have to reward it when it happens. The first time they challenge you and you say "good catch," that sets the norm. The first time you dismiss it, the system dies.

3. Write the decision before the meeting. A written decision you haven't shared yet is still revisable. A spoken one becomes gravity.

None of them ask you to slow down. They just insert a half-second of friction at the exact moment where unchecked momentum is most dangerous — right before the answer leaves your mouth.
